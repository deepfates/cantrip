# Mythic Mode

**By Valentine**, posted 23 Feb 2018 on LessWrong

*Note: This article was fetched via the GreaterWrong mirror due to LessWrong rate limiting. The content below represents the retrieved text.*

---

This essay explores using narrative frameworks intentionally to navigate cultural roles and personal transformation.

Valentine begins by referencing Scott Alexander's "Meditations on Moloch," which personified coordination failures as an eldritch deity. The author argues this essay succeeded not through conceptual novelty but through transposing existential risk discourse "into the key of myth."

The piece introduces the concept that "classical gods are real" when viewed through mythic mode--not as supernatural entities but as "distributed subroutines within the web of Omega." The author references Eric S. Raymond's essay discussing how archetypes and psychological complexes can be evoked through ritual and symbolism to influence human behavior and creativity.

## Core Concept

Valentine argues that culture functions as distributed intelligence, and individuals operate as nodes within this network. She introduces "mythic mode"--viewing the world through story-like lenses where people recognize themselves as characters in larger narratives. The author emphasizes this differs from mere metaphor; it involves experiencing stories from within rather than analyzing them externally.

## Key Arguments

### On Narrative Power

Valentine contends that "facts can inform culture, but story guides it." Scott Alexander's "Meditations on Moloch" exemplifies how transposing complex ideas into mythic language creates cultural impact by making abstract concepts tangible and emotionally resonant.

### On Role Transition

The essay presents a puzzle: defying an assigned social role typically reinforces it. However, stories contain patterns of role transition--the hero's journey and similar arcs--that suggest where "the web" permits scripts to shift. By identifying resonant story types matching one's desired transition and possessing sufficient slack, individuals might leverage these patterns.

Valentine addresses the core puzzle: if you're trapped in a cultural script, defying it typically reinforces your assigned role. However, well-known story archetypes (like the hero's journey) suggest structured paths for deep role transitions. The author proposes that entering mythic mode--experiencing narrative from inside rather than analyzing it externally--enables working with these patterns.

### On Epistemic Integrity

Valentine stresses that sandboxing mythic mode is crucial. One must clearly demarcate entry and exit points, recognizing that meaningful coincidences within mythic mode likely reflect confirmation bias. Exiting the framework allows conventional reasoning to examine what occurred.

The essay emphasizes the importance of "sandboxing" mythic mode: treating it as a contained framework separate from one's normal epistemic practices. The author states: "you can't reach and program the unconscious mind" through "sterile denotative language," requiring instead symbolic, emotional, and ritualistic approaches.

## Personal Example

After experiencing kensho (Zen enlightenment), Valentine sought a Rinzai Zen teacher but found none nearby. Switching to mythic mode, she recognized the warrior-monk archetype fit better than pure contemplative paths. This reframing led her to the Shaolin Monastery tradition and eventually to New York City, where she arrived the day the resident monk returned from Argentina--a synchronicity she frames as either orchestrated by the social web or confirmation bias, remaining uncertain which.

The author notes this could reflect confirmation bias or genuine synchronicity within distributed social computation. Attempting to correct for this while in mythic mode undermines the approach. The solution is exiting mythic mode afterward to assess what occurred with normal epistemology, keeping insights "contained within the sandbox."

## Controversies Explored

### Epistemic Risks

Critics worry mythic mode resembles "The Secret" and similar magical thinking. Sarah Constantine warns that believing the universe conspires to help when things succeed can terrify when misfortune strikes: "if the universe is conspiring to help you when things go right, you can also imagine that the universe is conspiring to hurt you when things go wrong, and that's terrifying." Valentine acknowledges the risk but argues that culture already shapes people through implicit narrative structures; explicitly using mythology with awareness differs from unconscious entanglement.

### Power and Danger

Cousin_it expresses concern that myth, while powerful, isn't asymmetric--falsehoods can use it as effectively as truth. Valentine responds that she wants to develop skill recognizing and countering mythic language precisely because its influence pervades culture regardless of one's participation.

### Narrative vs. Reality

Moridinamael warns against "reasoning by proximity to Protagonist Feelings," noting he's driven himself into problems by following compelling narrative logic. Valentine acknowledges this risk while maintaining that understanding narrative structure provides tools for working with rather than against cultural forces.

## The Sandboxing Framework

Valentine emphasizes that mythic mode must remain clearly bounded. One inhabits the framework temporarily, gathers insights, then exits to subject findings to ordinary reasoning. This prevents mythic thinking from colonizing all cognition while preserving its psychological benefits.

Valentine addresses rationalist community skepticism about mythic mode, suggesting that avoiding such powerful tools leaves people vulnerable to unconscious cultural influence anyway. The author proposes that learning to sandbox and use mythic mode intentionally is preferable to denying its existence while remaining subject to it.

The essay concludes that while not everyone should use mythic mode, those claiming nobody should use it will find that effort "a particular flavor of frustrating."

### Charlie Steiner

Charlie Steiner argues that beloved ideas are dangerous because people love them, not because they're useful, and that mythic mode might push culture toward spreading ideas based on emotional appeal over abstract value.

### Vanessa Kosoy's Paradox

Vanessa Kosoy raises a paradox: How do we decide what role we want if our reasoning about roles is itself part of our current role? Is there a "true self" independent of social positioning?

### On Western Solutions

Habryka argues Western approaches to mental health (antidepressants, scientific method) substantially outperform traditional alternatives, citing data suggesting Western cultures report less anxiety than Asian ones. Cousin_it presents contrary research suggesting clinical depression rates in the West are 4-10 times higher than in Asia.

### On Usefulness

The post sparked debate about whether mythic frameworks should primarily serve intuition pumps and cultural events versus enabling genuine behavioral change. Qiaochu_Yuan argues behavior modification often requires emotional and narrative engagement beyond evidence-based argumentation alone.

### FireItself on Sandboxing

FireItself asks for more details on how to sandbox as a human, prompting Valentine to explain that frameworks with mutual critiques can be separated by emphasizing boundaries between them and using mental triggers to track which mode one occupies.

The discussion demonstrates significant community tension between those viewing mythic mode as a valuable tool for behavioral change and those seeing it as an epistemic hazard.
